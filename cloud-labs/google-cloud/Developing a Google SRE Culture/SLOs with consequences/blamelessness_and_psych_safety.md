# Blamelessness and Psychological Safety in SRE

Site Reliability Engineers (SREs) believe that **failure is normal**. Progress comes from **learning from mistakes** and ensuring that the same mistake does not occur again in the same way.

A core SRE practice is conducting a **postmortem after an incident or outage**.

An **SRE postmortem is blameless**, meaning incidents are analyzed objectively **without assigning blame to individuals or teams**.

Blamelessness has a powerful impact on organizational culture because it helps create **psychological safety**.

---

# Psychological Safety

**Psychological safety** is the belief that a person will **not be punished or humiliated** for speaking up with:

- ideas
- questions
- concerns
- mistakes

Consider a situation where you had concerns about a task or decision in your team.

- Did you **speak up**?
- Or did you **stay quiet**?

In workplaces with **low psychological safety**, people often stay silent because they fear:

- looking incompetent
- appearing ignorant
- being ridiculed for having a different opinion

This fear can have long-lasting negative effects on teams.

---

# Consequences of Low Psychological Safety

Low psychological safety can **stifle learning and innovation**.

When people stay silent:

- Questions remain unanswered
- Ideas are never explored
- Learning opportunities disappear

Sometimes a question may have a simple answer. Other times, it might:

- spark new discussions
- inspire new ideas
- challenge assumptions

When employees focus on **managing impressions instead of sharing ideas**, the organization stops improving.

---

# How Leaders Can Build Psychological Safety

Leaders can take several simple steps:

### 1. Frame Work as a Learning Problem
Treat challenges as opportunities to **learn**, not simply as execution failures.

### 2. Encourage Every Voice
Make it clear that **everyone's input matters**.

### 3. Acknowledge Your Own Fallibility
Admit that you may miss things and **encourage others to question and contribute**.

### 4. Model Curiosity
Ask questions yourself and show that **curiosity is valued**.

---

# Impact on Software Delivery

Research shows that psychologically safe environments significantly improve software delivery.

In these environments:

- Collaboration is encouraged
- Cooperation between teams increases
- Messengers delivering bad news are not punished
- Failures are treated as learning opportunities
- New ideas are welcomed

These qualities lead to improvements in:

- **Lead time**
- **Deployment frequency**
- **Time to restore service**

Psychological safety strengthens **SRE team dynamics** and improves **operational excellence**.

# Understanding Blamelessness

Blamelessness is essential for effective postmortems.

It focuses on **understanding system failures rather than blaming individuals**.

But why do people blame others?

Research identifies two major reasons.

---

# Causes of Blame

## 1. Hindsight Bias

Hindsight bias occurs when people believe an outcome **should have been predictable**, even though it wasn't.

Example:

Someone claims they *knew* a sports team would lose **after the result is already known**.

In engineering environments, hindsight bias can lead to statements like:

> "They should have seen it coming."

This creates unfair blame toward the person responsible.

---

## 2. Discomfort Discharge

Blame can also be a way to **release emotional discomfort**.

Some researchers argue that humans are naturally wired to blame others as a way to cope with stress or frustration.

However, blaming individuals leads to harmful behaviors:

- People hide information
- Incidents go unreported
- Questions are avoided
- Root causes remain undiscovered

Mistakes only become valuable learning opportunities when **systemic causes are properly identified**.

---

# Blameless Postmortems

Blamelessness shifts responsibility from **people** to **systems and processes**.

In organizations that focus on blame, the first question after an incident is often:

> "Who caused this?"

This creates a culture of fear where employees become risk-averse.

A healthier approach assumes:

- Individuals act **in good faith**
- Decisions are made using the **best information available at the time**

Instead of blaming people, organizations should investigate:

- system design
- processes
- misleading information
- operational constraints

The goal is to **improve the system**, not punish individuals.

---

# Why Blame Hurts Innovation

Blame culture also harms innovation.

Innovation requires **risk-taking**, and no new product or process has a **100% chance of success**.

If employees fear punishment for failure:

- they stop proposing new ideas
- improvement slows
- creativity disappears

---

# Real-World Example

Google's Professional Services teams have worked with organizations transitioning to SRE culture.

For example, a major **online retailer in the Netherlands** learned about psychological safety during an SRE workshop.

Initially, their culture focused on **identifying someone to blame after incidents**.

Through:

- internal training
- SRE culture workshops
- performance management changes

they began shifting toward a **psychologically safe environment**.

---

# Key Takeaway

Blamelessness and psychological safety are **critical cultural foundations of SRE**.

Successful adoption of SRE depends on:

- learning from failures
- focusing on systems rather than individuals
- encouraging open communication
- fostering trust within teams

Organizations that commit to these cultural norms create environments where **teams learn faster, innovate more, and operate more reliably**.
